<h1>Goalkeeper Warm-Up Before a Match: 10 Minutes to the First Save</h1>

<p>A goalkeeper cannot afford to warm up during the match itself. The first tough save can come in the very first minute. That is why the warm-up must have a clear purpose — simply jogging around and handling a few balls is not enough.</p>

<p>A proper goalkeeper warm-up prepares both the body and the mind. It gets the legs moving, activates the posture, wakes up the hands and gives the goalkeeper confidence in contact with the ball. Most importantly, it sets a tempo that is close to match intensity.</p>

<p>The common mistake is simple. The outfield players do their block. The goalkeeper waits to the side, then quickly takes a few shots from the edge of the box. It looks busy. In reality, it usually prepares nothing but confusion.</p>

<p>The point of a warm-up is not to tire the goalkeeper. It is to prepare them for the first real situation — posture, footwork, first contact with the ball, communication and spatial awareness.</p>

<p>In this article you will find a straightforward ten-minute plan. It works for children, youth players and amateur goalkeepers. Each block can be adapted for age and level.</p>

<h2>What a Goalkeeper Warm-Up Must Achieve</h2>

<p>The warm-up has three clear tasks. First, raise physical readiness. Second, get the goalkeeper into the correct technical habits. Third, switch the mind from rest mode into match mode.</p>

<p>This is especially important for younger goalkeepers. They often arrive for a match full of energy but without rhythm. The first shot surprises them — not because it was unsaveable, but because they were not yet settled in posture and tempo.</p>

<p>A systematic review by Hammami and colleagues summarised in the Journal of Sports Medicine and Physical Fitness (2018) confirms that a well-designed dynamic warm-up in football demonstrably supports performance in speed and power activities. For a goalkeeper, the effect is visible immediately: a better first step, better footwork and better timing of movement.</p>

<p>But structure is essential. Going straight into difficult shots without preparation means the hands and body are often late. The goalkeeper then spends the first phase of the match searching for confidence that should already be there.</p>

<p>A good block therefore progresses from simple to complex — from movement without the ball through basic ball contact to situations that resemble real match moments. The FA Boot Room coaching guide "Countdown to Kick-Off" by Ian Bateman makes exactly this recommendation: progressive intensity and specificity throughout matchday preparation.</p>

<h2>How Many Minutes Is Enough?</h2>

<p>In most amateur and youth settings, ten minutes is enough. Sometimes even less. It depends on the weather, the age of the players, the previous schedule and the space around the pitch.</p>

<p>A twenty-minute block is not necessary just to look professional. A short, precise warm-up beats a long, scattered one.</p>

<p>For younger players, short intervals work best — quick rotation, clear instruction, fast feedback. With older youth players you can increase intensity and add more complex decision-making. The logic still applies: each block must prepare the one that follows.</p>

<p>One simple rule: after the warm-up, the goalkeeper should feel activated, confident and alert. Not so out of breath that they are still recovering when the referee blows for kick-off.</p>

<p>In cold weather, allow for a longer movement-only introduction. In the heat, keep blocks shorter and more precise. The warm-up prepares — it does not exhaust. That rule is worth following in every age group.</p>

<h2>A Ten-Minute Pre-Match Plan</h2>

<p><strong>Minutes 1–2: Leg activation and posture</strong></p>
<p>Start without the ball. Short hops, shuffle steps, lateral movements, low posture. Arms active in front of the body. The goal is not maximum speed — it is reminding the body of the basic goalkeeper frame.</p>

<p><strong>Minutes 3–4: Simple ball contact</strong></p>
<p>Light throws to the centre of the body, to the sides and above the shoulders. The ball should go cleanly into the hands. This is where the goalkeeper builds feel for the first touch. The coach checks that the goalkeeper is not flaring the elbows or falling unnecessarily backwards — and this block ties directly into <a href="https://bu1sport.com/blogs/magazine/how-to-train-goalkeeper-reaction">how to train goalkeeper reaction</a>, since an active first step is the foundation of both.</p>

<p><strong>Minutes 5–6: Footwork before the save</strong></p>
<p>The goalkeeper makes two to three short movements between markers or cones, then deals with a simple save. This is an important transition. A match does not begin with a shot from a standing start — it begins with movement before the save.</p>

<p><strong>Minutes 7–8: Low balls and first step</strong></p>
<p>Send several balls along the ground to the sides. Not too hard. The goalkeeper gets down cleanly. Monitor the first step, the angle of the body and composure when gathering the ball.</p>

<p><strong>Minute 9: High balls and timing</strong></p>
<p>Add a few balls in the air — they do not need to be difficult crosses. A high ball into safe space is enough. The goalkeeper reactivates their judgement of flight and timing of the hands.</p>

<p><strong>Minute 10: Two to three match-like situations</strong></p>
<p>Finish with a short block that resembles real action — a step to the side followed by a shot, or a simple rebound situation. This closes the warm-up at a tempo that already feels like a match.</p>

<h2>What to Focus on with Young Goalkeepers</h2>

<p>With younger goalkeepers, clarity is the key. Do not pile on five instructions at once. One emphasis per set is enough — for example, posture, hands in front of the body, or the first step.</p>

<p>With children it is also important that the warm-up builds confidence. When a coach starts blasting close-range shots just to wake the goalkeeper up quickly, the opposite often happens — the young goalkeeper tenses up and starts catching with their eyes rather than their body.</p>

<p>With older youth players you can increase tempo, add decision-making, a movement before the save, two options for finishing, a simple screened ball. Quality must always be preserved though.</p>

<p>The warm-up is not a competition for the hardest save before kick-off. It is preparation for performance. That is a big difference.</p>

<h2>The Most Common Mistakes Before Kick-Off</h2>

<p><strong>Mistake 1: Starting too fast.</strong></p>
<p>The goalkeeper goes straight into difficult shots. The body is not ready. Posture breaks down. The first contact lacks confidence.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 2: No footwork.</strong></p>
<p>The goalkeeper catches from a standing start. In the match they wonder why they cannot get to the ball after moving. A warm-up without feet is only half a warm-up.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 3: Too many close-range shots.</strong></p>
<p>This is a common bad habit. It looks energetic. In reality it rarely builds the right positioning or confidence.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 4: No communication.</strong></p>
<p>The goalkeeper quietly goes through their shots and steps into the match voiceless. But talking should start during the warm-up — calling, organising space.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 5: Poorly prepared gloves.</strong></p>

<h2>How the Warm-Up Should Connect to the Team</h2>

<p>The goalkeeper warm-up should not exist in a separate world from the rest of the squad. It should flow naturally into the team's routine. If the defence is working on build-up play or set-piece positioning, it makes sense for the goalkeeper to receive similar cues in the final part of their warm-up — so that the link to <a href="https://bu1sport.com/blogs/magazine/goalkeeper-distribution-with-feet">goalkeeper distribution with feet</a> comes naturally from the first minute of the match.</p>

<p>There is a mental benefit too. The goalkeeper enters the match at the same rhythm as the team. That helps especially with younger goalkeepers who sometimes struggle to find their match tempo after an isolated individual warm-up.</p>

<h2>What the Coach or Parent Should Do</h2>

<p>Not every team has a specialist goalkeeper coach. That does not matter. A coach, assistant or parent can run a quality warm-up by following a simple structure and not chasing spectacular moments.</p>

<p>Speak calmly. Do not give ten instructions in one minute. Make sure the warm-up leads from confidence to intensity, not the other way around. The FA Boot Room futsal goalkeeper technical warm-up session is a useful reference — the principles translate directly to eleven-a-side football.</p>

<p>The goalkeeper needs a few clean contacts before a match, a few movements in posture and a few situations that remind them of timing. That is more than enough if the quality is there.</p>

<h2>How Do You Know the Warm-Up Worked?</h2>

<p>The signal is simple. The goalkeeper is ready for the first moment. Not stiff. Not over-stimulated. They do not deal with the first save in a panic. They do not play the first distribution blindly. They look calm and confident.</p>

<p>That is what the warm-up is for. Not to create a spectacle for spectators. But to prepare the goalkeeper for the first important moment.</p>

<p>This holds on smaller pitches and in lower divisions too, where there is often less space and less time. A quality warm-up is not about the number of cones and balls. It shows when the goalkeeper knows what they are doing, why they are doing it and at what tempo they want to enter the match.</p>
